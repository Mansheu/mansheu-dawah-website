WHAT ISLAM SAYS ABOUT...

Hajj and Umrah

Share:

Hajj is the major pilgrimage to the sacred city of Makkah that every adult Muslim must perform once in a lifetime if physically and financially able.

It takes place in the days of Dhul-Hijjah, the final month of the Islamic lunar calendar, and traces the legacy of Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham), Lady Hajar, and their son Isma'il.

Umrah is a shorter pilgrimage to Makkah that can be performed any time of the year and consists mainly of ihram, tawaf around the Ka'bah, and Sa'i between Safa and Marwah.

While it is not obligatory, the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) encouraged Muslims to perform Umrah as often as they can because it expiates sins between one Umrah and the next.

These pilgrimages renew one's covenant with Allah, cultivate humility, and connect the believer to the history of tawhid (pure monotheism).

Hajj is an obligation and a pillar of Islam, while Umrah is a powerful voluntary act that keeps the heart attached to the sacred sanctuary.

Hajj becomes obligatory once in a lifetime for every Muslim who is sane, mature, physically capable, and financially able to travel while leaving sufficient support for dependents.

Debts and immediate obligations should be settled first and women may require a mahram (male guardian) depending on the scholarly opinion they follow and travel conditions.

Hajj is performed only during the 8th to the 13th days of Dhul-Hijjah, the twelfth month of the Islamic lunar calendar.

The climax of the pilgrimage occurs on the 9th of Dhul-Hijjah when pilgrims stand on the plains of Arafah.

Hajj is a pillar performed during specific days, whereas Umrah is a recommended visit that can be made year-round.

  • Hajj contains extra rites: standing at Arafah, spending the night in Muzdalifah, and stoning the Jamarat.
  • Umrah includes entering ihram, performing tawaf, praying two units, completing Sa'i, and shaving or trimming the hair.
  • Skipping Hajj without a valid excuse is sinful; skipping Umrah is not.

Ihram refers to the sacred state entered before beginning Hajj or Umrah and it requires special garments, intentions, and etiquette.

  • Men wear two unstitched white cloths, while women wear modest dress that covers the body.
  • Ghusl or wudu is made, fragrance is applied (men only), and the pilgrim declares the intention with the Talbiyah at the miqat.
  • From that moment, prohibitions such as clipping nails, using perfume, or marital intimacy take effect until the state of ihram is exited.

The Talbiyah - 'Labbayka Allahumma labbayk...' - is the pilgrim's declaration of answering Allah's call and it is repeated aloud after entering ihram until the start of stoning the Jamarat.

Its continuous recitation softens the heart, keeps the tongue engaged in remembrance, and emphasizes the exclusive servitude owed to Allah alone.

Hajj is performed through a sequence of rites that symbolize obedience, sacrifice, and unity.

  1. Entering ihram and reciting the Talbiyah.
  2. Performing tawaf and Sa'i upon arriving in Makkah (for Tamattu').
  3. Spending the 8th in Mina, standing at Arafah on the 9th, and staying overnight in Muzdalifah.
  4. Stoning the pillars, offering an animal sacrifice, shaving or cutting hair, and making Tawaf al-Ifadah.
  5. Completing the remaining days of stoning before performing Tawaf al-Wada when departing.

Sa'i is walking seven times between the hills of Safa and Marwah in remembrance of Hajar's desperate search for water for infant Isma'il.

It teaches perseverance, hope, and total reliance on Allah when help seems distant, culminating in the blessing of the Zamzam spring.

Standing at Arafah from noon until sunset on the 9th of Dhul-Hijjah is the single most important pillar of Hajj.

The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said, 'Hajj is Arafah,' highlighting that heartfelt supplication, repentance, and seeking forgiveness on this day are the essence of the entire pilgrimage.

Muzdalifah and Mina are sacred sites where pilgrims rest, pray, and complete symbolic acts of remembrance.

At Muzdalifah, pilgrims collect pebbles and spend the night under the open sky after combining Maghrib and Isha prayers. In Mina, they stone the three pillars across three days and stay overnight to continue dhikr and takbir.

Stoning the Jamarat commemorates Prophet Ibrahim's rejection of Satan's whispers when he was commanded to sacrifice his son.

Throwing the pebbles reminds pilgrims to cast away temptation, arrogance, and despair whenever they arise in their own lives.

Tawaf al-Ifadah is a mandatory circumambulation performed after the Day of Sacrifice, while Tawaf al-Wada is the farewell circumambulation made before leaving Makkah.

The first completes the essential rites of Hajj, and the second keeps the Ka'bah as the last sight a pilgrim carries in their heart when departing.

Preparation begins months in advance with sincere repentance, knowledge, and halal savings.

  • Study the rites with scholars or reputable guides and rehearse du'a lists.
  • Seek forgiveness from family and repay debts to travel with a clean conscience.
  • Budget responsibly for travel packages, emergency funds, and charity while ensuring obligatory financial duties such as zakat are met.

Women complete all rites just like men while maintaining modest dress, personal space, and group travel protocols.

Families benefit from traveling with trusted packages, setting meeting points, keeping hydration and health supplies handy, and designating caretakers for children or elders to avoid separation in large crowds.

The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) taught that an accepted Hajj erases all previous sins and that one Umrah to the next wipes out what occurs between them.

Hajj Mabroor (an accepted Hajj) has no reward except Paradise because it reflects sincere devotion free from showmanship or disobedience.

Those unable to travel should keep the intention alive, save patiently, and engage in deeds that mimic the spirit of the pilgrimage.

Fasting on the day of Arafah, giving charity to sponsor other pilgrims, studying the biography of Prophet Ibrahim, and visiting local mosques frequently all nurture the longing to one day answer the call of Allah in person.

What Islam Says About...

Basics of Islam

Islam is a monotheistic faith founded on the belief in one God (Allah) and the teachings of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). Muslims follow the Five Pillars of Islam and believe in the Quran as the final revelation from God.

Ramadan

Ramadan is the ninth month of the Islamic calendar when Muslims fast from dawn to sunset. It is a time of spiritual reflection, prayer, charity, and community gathering, culminating in the celebration of Eid al-Fitr.

Shariah

Shariah is Islamic law derived from the Quran and Sunnah. It provides guidance on worship, ethics, family life, and social justice. Shariah emphasizes compassion, fairness, and the protection of human dignity in all aspects of life.

Health & Wellness

Islam teaches that the body is a trust from Allah and must be cared for properly. Muslims are encouraged to maintain physical health through balanced nutrition, exercise, and cleanliness, while also nurturing mental and spiritual well-being.

Charity

Charity is a fundamental principle in Islam. Zakat (obligatory charity) is one of the Five Pillars, while Sadaqah (voluntary charity) is highly encouraged. Giving purifies wealth and helps create a just and compassionate society.

Peace & Violence

Islam promotes peace, justice, and the sanctity of human life. The religion condemns terrorism and unjust violence. True jihad is primarily a spiritual struggle for self-improvement, while physical defense is only permitted in specific, limited circumstances.

Women

Islam grants women rights to education, property ownership, and spiritual equality with men. Women are honored as mothers, daughters, wives, and professionals. Islamic teachings emphasize respect, dignity, and protection for women in all spheres of life.

Men

Men in Islam are called to be protectors and maintainers of their families with justice and compassion. Islamic masculinity emphasizes responsibility, humility, emotional intelligence, and service to family and community while striving for personal spiritual growth.
