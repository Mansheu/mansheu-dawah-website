WHAT ISLAM SAYS ABOUT...

Shariah

Share:

Shariah literally means a path to water and refers to the holistic guidance Allah revealed for all aspects of life.

It encompasses worship, ethics, social dealings, economic justice, and governance grounded in revelation.

The Qur'an and the authentic Sunnah of the Prophet ? are the foundational sources.

Jurists also employ consensus (ijma'), analogical reasoning (qiyas), and other scholarly tools rooted in these texts.

Shariah is the divine guidance itself, while fiqh is the human understanding and application of that guidance.

Because fiqh involves interpretation, schools of thought may differ while still remaining within Shariah.

Shariah aims to preserve faith, life, intellect, family, and wealth, securing benefit and preventing harm.

These objectives help scholars evaluate new circumstances and prioritize mercy and justice.

Core beliefs and rituals are fixed, yet Shariah provides flexible mechanisms for changing contexts.

Principles such as necessity, public interest, and local custom allow scholars to respond to emerging realities.

Qualified scholars (ulama) trained in Qur'an, Hadith, Arabic, and legal principles interpret and convey rulings.

They issue fatwas (non-binding legal opinions) after understanding the questioner's circumstances.

The Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali schools represent preserved methodologies for deriving rulings.

Their differences reflect diverse evidence and reasoning, offering richness rather than division.

Hudud are rarely applied fixed penalties with strict evidentiary standards designed to safeguard society.

They represent only a tiny portion of Shariah, which primarily emphasizes worship, ethics, and social welfare.

By preserving life, dignity, property, and freedom of belief, Shariah establishes universal rights grounded in divine accountability.

Islamic governance historically provided legal recourse even for non-Muslim citizens.

They follow local laws while faithfully practicing personal aspects of Shariah like prayer, charity, and family ethics.

Scholars develop fiqh al-aqalliyat (minority jurisprudence) to address civic participation and contracts ethically.

Many Muslim-majority countries blend Shariah-based personal law with civil or common-law frameworks.

The goal is to harmonize timeless values with contemporary governance and human development.

It grants women rights to education, inheritance, financial independence, consent in marriage, and protection from abuse.

Historical Muslim societies produced leading female scholars, jurists, and businesswomen operating under Shariah.

It prohibits interest (riba), fraud, and exploitation while encouraging risk-sharing, fair wages, and zakat.

Islamic finance seeks ethical investments that align profit with social responsibility.

Shura (consultation) is an essential Shariah principle requiring leaders to seek counsel and community input.

It underpins participatory governance, transparency, and accountability.

Safeguarding life obligates Muslim societies to invest in healthcare, sanitation, and environmental stewardship.

Prophetic teachings forbid polluting water sources and encourage planting trees even during crises.

Recognized local customs can be incorporated into rulings as long as they do not contradict revelation.

This principle keeps Shariah relevant to diverse cultures and eras.

Respond with knowledge, civility, and examples of Shariah's mercy rather than defensiveness.

Sharing authentic resources and building relationships dispels stereotypes far better than arguing online.

Obeying divine law purifies the heart, disciplines the soul, and brings a believer closer to Allah.

When Muslims view Shariah as guidance—not merely law—they experience tranquility, purpose, and hope.

What Islam Says About...

Basics of Islam

Islam is a monotheistic faith founded on the belief in one God (Allah) and the teachings of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). Muslims follow the Five Pillars of Islam and believe in the Quran as the final revelation from God.

Hajj and Umrah

Hajj is the annual pilgrimage to Mecca that every able-bodied Muslim must perform at least once in their lifetime. Umrah is a shorter pilgrimage performed year-round for spiritual closeness to Allah.

Ramadan

Ramadan is the ninth month of the Islamic calendar when Muslims fast from dawn to sunset. It is a time of spiritual reflection, prayer, charity, and community gathering, culminating in the celebration of Eid al-Fitr.

Health & Wellness

Islam teaches that the body is a trust from Allah and must be cared for properly. Muslims are encouraged to maintain physical health through balanced nutrition, exercise, and cleanliness, while also nurturing mental and spiritual well-being.

Charity

Charity is a fundamental principle in Islam. Zakat (obligatory charity) is one of the Five Pillars, while Sadaqah (voluntary charity) is highly encouraged. Giving purifies wealth and helps create a just and compassionate society.

Peace & Violence

Islam promotes peace, justice, and the sanctity of human life. The religion condemns terrorism and unjust violence. True jihad is primarily a spiritual struggle for self-improvement, while physical defense is only permitted in specific, limited circumstances.

Women

Islam grants women rights to education, property ownership, and spiritual equality with men. Women are honored as mothers, daughters, wives, and professionals. Islamic teachings emphasize respect, dignity, and protection for women in all spheres of life.

Men

Men in Islam are called to be protectors and maintainers of their families with justice and compassion. Islamic masculinity emphasizes responsibility, humility, emotional intelligence, and service to family and community while striving for personal spiritual growth.
