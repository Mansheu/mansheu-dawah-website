Racism and Islam: A Faith That Transcends Color

Examining Islam's revolutionary stance on racial equality, from the Prophet Muhammad's final sermon to contemporary Muslim communities' ongoing struggle against prejudice.

Authored by Ust. Sheu Babakar

Published: December 16, 2025 • Jumada al-Akhirah 16, 1447

Updated: December 16, 2025 • Jumada al-Akhirah 16, 1447

Read time: 14 min

In the 7th century Arabian Peninsula, where tribal lineage and skin color determined worth, Islam emerged with a revolutionary message: all humans are equal before God. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, in his final sermon, declared: "All mankind is from Adam and Eve. An Arab has no superiority over a non-Arab, nor does a non-Arab have any superiority over an Arab; a white has no superiority over a black, nor does a black have any superiority over a white—except by piety and good action." This statement dismantled centuries of entrenched racism.

Yet today, Muslim communities worldwide grapple with racial prejudice that contradicts Islamic teachings. From colorism in South Asian Muslim families to anti-Black racism in Arab societies, from ethnic tensions in Southeast Asian communities to discrimination against converts in immigrant Muslim populations—racism persists where it should be unthinkable. This article examines Islam's unequivocal stance against racism and challenges Muslims to align their practice with their faith.

The Quranic Foundation of Equality

The Quran establishes human dignity as inherent, not contingent on race, ethnicity, or status. Allah declares in Surah Al-Hujurat (49:13): "O mankind, indeed We have created you from male and female and made you peoples and tribes that you may know one another. Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous of you." This verse affirms that diversity is by design—so people may appreciate different cultures and learn from one another—but superiority is determined solely by taqwa (God-consciousness), which Allah alone can judge.

The Quran repeatedly emphasizes the unity of humanity's origin. In Surah An-Nisa (4:1), Allah reminds believers: "O mankind, fear your Lord, who created you from one soul and created from it its mate and dispersed from both of them many men and women." By tracing all humans to a single origin, the Quran dismantles any basis for racial hierarchy. We are one human family, differentiated only in superficial characteristics that should inspire curiosity and respect, not prejudice.

Classical scholars such as Imam Ibn Taymiyyah and Imam al-Ghazali emphasized that tribalism and racism (asabiyyah) rooted in arrogance contradict Islamic brotherhood. The believer sees others through the lens of faith and character, not through the distorting prism of race.

The Prophet's Revolutionary Stand

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ didn't merely preach equality—he embodied it. He elevated Bilal ibn Rabah, a formerly enslaved African man, to the position of the first muadhin (caller to prayer), one of Islam's most honored roles. When some Arabs objected, questioning how a Black man could hold such status, the Prophet ﷺ rebuked them, saying Bilal was among the best of people. This wasn't tokenism; it was a deliberate dismantling of racial supremacy.

The Prophet ﷺ appointed Usama ibn Zayd, a young man whose father was a freed slave, to lead an army that included senior Arab companions. When some complained, the Prophet ﷺ defended Usama and praised his leadership qualities, demonstrating that merit, not lineage or color, determines authority in Islam.

During his final pilgrimage, the Prophet ﷺ delivered the Farewell Sermon to over 100,000 Muslims. In that historic address, he explicitly condemned racism: "All mankind is from Adam and Eve... All of you are equal. Nobody has superiority over another except by piety and good action." He made the companions repeat after him and asked them to convey this message to those absent, ensuring this principle would be preserved for all generations.

When Abu Dharr insulted Bilal by referencing his mother's race, the Prophet ﷺ rebuked him sharply: "You are a man who still has Jahiliyyah (pre-Islamic ignorance) in him!" Abu Dharr, one of the most righteous companions, immediately repented and placed his face on the ground, asking Bilal to step on it as penance. This incident demonstrates that even great companions were held accountable for racist remarks, and that sincere repentance requires humility and transformation.

Historical Impact: Breaking Social Hierarchies

Early Islamic civilization witnessed unprecedented racial integration. The Abbasid Caliphate employed scholars, generals, and administrators from diverse backgrounds—Arab, Persian, Turkish, African, and Berber. The famed general Tariq ibn Ziyad, who led the Muslim conquest of Spain, was a Berber. Ibn Sina (Avicenna), one of history's greatest physicians and philosophers, was Persian. Imam Malik, founder of the Maliki school of jurisprudence, had Ethiopian ancestry.

The Islamic slave trade, while morally problematic by modern standards, operated under rules that sharply contrasted with later transatlantic slavery. Islam mandated humane treatment, prohibited separating families, encouraged manumission, and granted freed slaves full social equality. Many former slaves rose to positions of political and intellectual prominence. The Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt was ruled by former slave-soldiers who became defenders of Islam during the Crusades and Mongol invasions.

Muslim Spain (Al-Andalus) exemplified this cosmopolitanism. For centuries, Muslims, Christians, and Jews of various ethnic backgrounds coexisted, collaborated in science and philosophy, and created one of history's most advanced civilizations. This pluralism, rooted in Islamic principles of human dignity, stands in stark contrast to the racial segregation that characterized other societies.

Contemporary Reality: Racism in Muslim Communities

Despite Islam's clear teachings, racism persists in Muslim communities worldwide. In South Asia, colorism remains entrenched; darker-skinned Muslims face discrimination in marriage proposals and social standing. Skin-lightening products are marketed aggressively, perpetuating the toxic belief that lighter skin is superior. This contradicts the Prophet's ﷺ example—he loved Aisha, described as fair-skinned, and Aisha loved Bilal, describing him as blessed despite Arab prejudice against his appearance.

In Arab countries, anti-Black racism manifests in how African Muslims are treated. Despite the Prophet ﷺ praising Bilal and marrying Safiyyah (whose mother was Ethiopian), some Arabs use derogatory terms for Black people and discriminate in housing, employment, and marriage. The 2020 murder of George Floyd prompted some Arab Muslims to reflect on their own racism, but systemic change remains slow.

In Western Muslim communities, ethnic cliques and national origin hierarchies divide the ummah. Pakistani, Arab, Somali, and Turkish Muslims often self-segregate. Converts, especially Black and Latino converts, report feeling marginalized in immigrant-dominated mosques. They are praised for accepting Islam but excluded from leadership and marriage pools—a contradiction that drives some away from community involvement.

Even within African Muslim communities, tribal prejudices and ethnic tensions mirror the jahiliyyah (ignorance) Islam came to eradicate. Muslims who would unite against external threats remain divided along ethnic and racial lines when dealing with each other.

Addressing Prejudice: A Call to Action

The first step is acknowledging the problem. Muslims must stop deflecting with statements like "Islam solved racism 1400 years ago" while tolerating racist attitudes in families and communities. Islam provided the solution; Muslims have failed to implement it fully. Honest self-examination is necessary: Do I harbor prejudice based on skin color, ethnicity, or national origin? Do I perpetuate these biases through my words, marriage preferences, or social circles?

Education is critical. Islamic schools and khutbahs (sermons) must regularly address racism, teaching children and adults the Prophet's ﷺ anti-racist teachings. Stories of Bilal, Salman al-Farsi (the Persian companion), and other diverse companions should be highlighted. Youth need role models who reflect Islam's diversity and who actively combat prejudice.

Parents must examine how they discuss race with children. Jokes, stereotypes, and preferences expressed at home shape children's worldviews. When parents forbid their children from marrying outside their ethnicity—citing "cultural compatibility"—they perpetuate racism disguised as tradition. The Prophet ﷺ said: "If there comes to you one whose religion and character pleases you, then marry him. If you do not, there will be tribulation and great corruption on earth" (Tirmidhi). Notice he mentioned religion and character—not race, ethnicity, or tribe.

Practical Steps Toward Racial Justice

In mosques: Ensure leadership reflects the community's diversity. Invite speakers of different ethnicities. Address racism explicitly in Friday sermons. Create welcoming committees that help newcomers and converts feel included. Offer cultural competency training for board members. Celebrate the ummah's diversity through events highlighting different Islamic cultures.

In families: Teach children that all humans are descendants of Adam and Eve. Challenge racist jokes and stereotypes immediately. Encourage friendships across ethnic lines. When discussing marriage, prioritize faith and character over ethnicity. Expose children to diverse Muslim scholars, writers, and role models through books and media.

In communities: Support Black Muslim voices and leadership. Attend anti-racism workshops and book discussions. Advocate for racially just policies in Muslim organizations. When witnessing racism, speak up—silence is complicity. Build coalitions with other marginalized groups to address systemic injustice.

Personally: Examine your biases through honest reflection and prayer. Diversify your friendships, reading lists, and social media follows. Attend lectures and events at mosques outside your ethnic community. Learn about the experiences of Muslims from different backgrounds. Make sincere tawbah (repentance) if you've harbored or expressed racist attitudes, and commit to active change.

Institutionally: Muslim schools should teach Islamic history's racial diversity. Islamic organizations must collect data on racial representation in leadership and programming. Nonprofits serving Muslim communities should ensure their services are accessible and culturally sensitive to all ethnic groups. Imams and scholars should regularly address racial justice as a fundamental Islamic principle, not an optional social issue.

Conclusion

Islam's anti-racist message was revolutionary in the 7th century and remains revolutionary today. In an era of resurgent white supremacy, anti-immigrant hatred, and colorism across cultures, Muslims have an obligation to embody the Prophet's ﷺ egalitarian vision. The ummah should be a beacon of racial harmony, demonstrating to the world that faith transcends color, ethnicity, and origin.

Yet this will not happen through platitudes. It requires intentional effort: confronting prejudice in ourselves and our communities, elevating marginalized voices, reforming exclusionary practices, and teaching the next generation that racism is not just un-Islamic—it is anti-Islamic. The Prophet ﷺ placed his face on the ground in prostration beside formerly enslaved companions, showing that before Allah, we are all equal. Can we do less?

The question for every Muslim is: Will I be part of perpetuating the racism that Islam came to eradicate, or will I be part of realizing the Prophet's ﷺ vision of a unified ummah where "an Arab has no superiority over a non-Arab, nor a non-Arab over an Arab, a white over a black, nor a black over a white—except by piety"? The answer must be reflected not in words, but in action.

May Allah purify our hearts from racism and grant us the courage to stand for justice. May He make us among those who recognize all humans as His creation, deserving of dignity, respect, and equal treatment. And may the Muslim ummah become the inclusive, diverse, and united community that Islam envisioned.
