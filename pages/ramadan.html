WHAT ISLAM SAYS ABOUT...

Ramadan

Share:

Ramadan is the ninth month of the Islamic lunar calendar in which healthy, adult Muslims fast from dawn to sunset as an act of worship.

It commemorates the beginning of Qur'anic revelation and is a season of heightened devotion, charity, and community connection.

Fasting cultivates taqwa (God-consciousness) by teaching self-restraint, gratitude, and empathy for the needy.

Allah commands fasting in Surah al-Baqarah 2:183 so believers may attain righteousness and purify their hearts.

Every Muslim who is mature, sane, physically able, and not experiencing a legitimate exemption must fast the entire month.

Children, the chronically ill, menstruating women, travelers, and pregnant or nursing mothers with valid concerns may delay or compensate their fasting.

Ramadan begins with the sighting of the new crescent moon or the completion of thirty days of Sha'ban.

Muslim communities follow local or global moon-sighting reports, and both approaches are acceptable within Islamic jurisprudence.

Eating, drinking, smoking, or intimate relations after dawn invalidate the fast.

  • Intentional vomiting or swallowing non-food items also counts.
  • Unintentional actions, such as swallowing saliva or eating forgetfully, do not break the fast.
  • Medical necessities like injections that nourish should be discussed with qualified scholars.

Suhoor, the pre-dawn meal, provides physical energy and carries immense prophetic blessings.

Delaying suhoor closer to Fajr while eating wholesome foods and hydrating well makes fasting more manageable.

Iftar should begin immediately at sunset with dates and water followed by a heartfelt du'a.

Sharing meals with family, neighbors, or masjid communities magnifies the reward.

Tarawih refers to the special night prayers offered in Ramadan after 'Isha to recite large portions of the Qur'an.

Whether prayed in the masjid or at home, it is a voluntary act that revives the Sunnah and builds spiritual stamina.

Laylat al-Qadr (the Night of Decree) is better than a thousand months of worship and marks the descent of the Qur'an.

Believers intensify du'a, Qur'an, and charity during the final ten nights to seek forgiveness and divine mercy.

Ramadan is the month in which the Qur'an was first revealed, making recitation and reflection central acts.

Many Muslims set completion goals, join study circles, or memorize passages to revive their relationship with Allah's words.

Zakat al-Fitr is a mandatory charity given before Eid prayer to purify the fast and feed the poor.

Every Muslim who possesses surplus food or wealth must contribute on behalf of themselves and their dependents.

Islam offers concessions, allowing them to postpone fasts and make them up later or feed the poor if recovery is unlikely.

Consulting reliable scholars and healthcare professionals helps tailor decisions to personal circumstances.

Women are prohibited from fasting during menstruation or post-partum bleeding and must make up those days later.

They can still engage in dhikr, du'a, charity, and serving others to maintain their spiritual momentum.

Introducing age-appropriate mini-fasts, decorating homes, and reading prophetic stories fosters love for the month.

Encouraging children to donate, prepare iftar, or memorize short du'as builds lasting habits of service.

Aligning work blocks with energy peaks, batching meetings, and napping briefly after Dhuhr preserves stamina.

Replacing doom scrolling with Qur'an breaks or dhikr walks keeps the day purposeful.

Consistency after Ramadan proves the sincerity of our efforts.

  • Maintain daily Qur'an recitation, even a page a day.
  • Continue fasting six days of Shawwal or the white days monthly.
  • Keep giving charity, attending circles of knowledge, and guarding the tongue.

Collective iftars, nightly prayers, and service projects knit hearts together in brotherhood and sisterhood.

Masjids often host food drives, counseling, and educational programs that continue throughout the year.

Eid al-Fitr celebrates the completion of Ramadan with communal prayers, family visits, and acts of gratitude.

It begins with ghusl, wearing one's best clothes, exchanging greetings, and appreciating Allah for guiding us to fast.

What Islam Says About...

Basics of Islam

Islam is a monotheistic faith founded on the belief in one God (Allah) and the teachings of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). Muslims follow the Five Pillars of Islam and believe in the Quran as the final revelation from God.

Hajj and Umrah

Hajj is the annual pilgrimage to Mecca that every able-bodied Muslim must perform at least once in their lifetime. Umrah is a shorter pilgrimage performed year-round for spiritual closeness to Allah.

Shariah

Shariah is Islamic law derived from the Quran and Sunnah. It provides guidance on worship, ethics, family life, and social justice. Shariah emphasizes compassion, fairness, and the protection of human dignity in all aspects of life.

Health & Wellness

Islam teaches that the body is a trust from Allah and must be cared for properly. Muslims are encouraged to maintain physical health through balanced nutrition, exercise, and cleanliness, while also nurturing mental and spiritual well-being.

Charity

Charity is a fundamental principle in Islam. Zakat (obligatory charity) is one of the Five Pillars, while Sadaqah (voluntary charity) is highly encouraged. Giving purifies wealth and helps create a just and compassionate society.

Peace & Violence

Islam promotes peace, justice, and the sanctity of human life. The religion condemns terrorism and unjust violence. True jihad is primarily a spiritual struggle for self-improvement, while physical defense is only permitted in specific, limited circumstances.

Women

Islam grants women rights to education, property ownership, and spiritual equality with men. Women are honored as mothers, daughters, wives, and professionals. Islamic teachings emphasize respect, dignity, and protection for women in all spheres of life.

Men

Men in Islam are called to be protectors and maintainers of their families with justice and compassion. Islamic masculinity emphasizes responsibility, humility, emotional intelligence, and service to family and community while striving for personal spiritual growth.
